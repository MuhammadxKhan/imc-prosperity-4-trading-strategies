## Implementation Notes & Limitations

The strategy is deliberately simple. Each product is compared against the average mid-price of its product family, adjusted by a fixed offset. If the product is far enough above this fair value, the strategy shorts it; if it is far enough below, the strategy buys it.

A few implementation choices mattered:

- Products are grouped into families of five, so the model trades relative mispricing rather than trying to forecast each product independently.
- Offsets are static rather than adaptive. I tested the idea of using live/EMA-style offsets, but this tended to move the fair value toward bad prices and weakened the signal.
- The strategy skips a whole family if one product has a missing or incomplete order book, since a partial family average can pollute every signal in that group.
- Only products with useful validation behaviour were traded; the rest were deliberately excluded.
- Orders are generated by moving toward a target position within the `-10` to `+10` position limit.

The main limitation is that the model assumes the relative relationships between products remain stable. If the product offsets shift during the round, the strategy can keep trading against stale fair values.

The position logic is also quite blunt: once a residual crosses its threshold, the strategy targets the full long or short position rather than scaling exposure with signal strength. Execution is aggressive too, because the code crosses visible liquidity to reach the target position rather than posting passive orders.

Given more time, I would test:

- rolling or regime-aware offsets that do not simply chase bad prices;
- z-score thresholds instead of raw residual thresholds;
- position sizing based on residual magnitude;
- explicit exit rules when residuals converge;
- passive execution variants to reduce spread costs.
